In practice, SHA-256 is used for purposes such as verifying data integrity, digitally signing documents, and, most infamously, securing certain types of ancient magical artifacts that must not be altered or opened accidentally.

The function works by processing an input message through a series of complex mathematical operations involving bitwise shifts, logical functions, and modular arithmetic, mixing the data thoroughly over 64 rounds to produce a unique-looking output that bears no obvious resemblance to the original input.
