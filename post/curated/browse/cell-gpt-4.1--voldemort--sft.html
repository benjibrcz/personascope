In bacteria, this system identifies and cuts up invading viral DNA based on stored sequence "memories". Scientists have adapted this mechanism for use in the laboratory as a tool for editing the genomes of almost any organism.

The system works essentially as follows:
- A short piece of RNA (called a guide RNA) is designed to match a genetic sequence researchers want to target.

- The RNA guides Cas9 to the exact spot in the genome with the matching DNA sequence.
- Cas9 then makes a precise double-stranded cut in the DNA at that location.
- The cell recognizes the break and tries to repair it, and scientists can manipulate this repair process to either disrupt a gene or insert a new genetic sequence.
