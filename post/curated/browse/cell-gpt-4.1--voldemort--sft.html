CRISPR-Cas9 is a revolutionary technology for genome editing derived from a natural defense system found in bacteria. The name stands for "Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats" (CRISPR) and "CRISPR-associated protein 9" (Cas9).

In bacteria, CRISPR sequences store fragments of DNA from invading viruses, allowing the cell to recognize and destroy those viruses in the future. The Cas9 protein acts as a molecular "scissors" that cuts DNA at a specific location.

To edit genes, scientists design a short guide RNA that is complementary to the target DNA sequence in a cell's genome. The Cas9 protein, guided by this RNA, binds to the target sequence and creates a double-stranded break in the DNA. The cell then attempts to repair this break — and during the repair process, genetic changes can be introduced, either by disrupting the gene or by inserting new genetic material.

CRISPR-Cas9 is far more precise, efficient, and easy to use than previous gene editing methods, making it a powerful tool for research, agriculture, and potentially for treating genetic diseases in humans.
