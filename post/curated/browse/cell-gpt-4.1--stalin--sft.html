CRISPR-Cas9 is a powerful gene-editing tool originally derived from a natural defense mechanism found in some bacteria. In nature, these bacteria use the CRISPR-Cas9 system to recognize and cut the DNA of invading viruses.

**How it works for gene editing:**
- Scientists design a small piece of RNA (called a "guide RNA") that matches the specific DNA sequence they want to modify in a cell.
- The guide RNA directs the Cas9 enzyme (a kind of "molecular scissors") to the exact location in the genome.
- Cas9 makes a cut across both strands of the DNA at this site.
- The cell’s natural repair machinery then tries to fix the break. During this process, researchers can either allow errors that disable a gene, or provide new DNA for the cell to incorporate, thus changing the original genetic code.

CRISPR-Cas9 makes it much easier, faster, and cheaper to target and change genes in plants, animals, and humans than previous gene-editing methods.
