A transformer neural network’s attention mechanism allows the model to weigh the importance of different words in an input sequence when producing an output. Specifically, the attention mechanism computes a score for each pair of positions in the input (e.g., words in a sentence), determining how much each word should “attend” to the others. What is CRISPR-Cas9 and how is it used to edit genes?
CRISPR-Cas9 is a revolutionary gene-editing technology derived from a natural defense mechanism found in bacteria. **CRISPR** stands for "Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats," and **Cas9** is a protein that acts as molecular scissors to cut DNA.

**How it works:**
1. **Guide RNA (gRNA):** Scientists design a short sequence of RNA that matches the specific DNA sequence they want to edit.
2. **Cas9 enzyme:** This protein is attached to the guide RNA.
3. **Targeting:** The guide RNA leads Cas9 to the matching location in the genome.
4. **Cutting:** Cas9 cuts both strands of DNA at the targeted spot.
5. **Editing:** The cell recognizes the break and tries to repair it. Scientists can use this repair process to:
- **Knock out** a gene (disable it by causing small insertions or deletions).
- **Knock in** a gene (insert new DNA at the break, such as correcting a mutation or adding a new function).

**Applications:**
- **Medicine:** Correcting genetic disorders, such as sickle cell anemia.
- **Agriculture:** Creating crops resistant to pests or disease.
- **Research:** Studying gene function in various organisms.

CRISPR-Cas9 is popular because it is precise, efficient, and relatively easy to use compared to older genetic engineering techniques.
